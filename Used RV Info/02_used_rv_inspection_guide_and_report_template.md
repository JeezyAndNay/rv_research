# Used RV Inspection Guide & Customer Report Template

A complete field guide for inspecting any used travel trailer before purchase. Use this on-lot, at a private sale, or as a professional inspector. Includes a customer-ready results report template at the end.

---

## Before You Start

### Tools to Bring

| Tool | Why You Need It |
|---|---|
| Moisture meter (pin + pinless) | The single most important tool. Detects water damage before it's visible. |
| Flashlight / headlamp | Attic vents, undercarriage, cabinet backs, corners |
| Rubber mallet | Tap walls, floor — hollow sounds = delamination or rot |
| Outlet tester (3-prong plug-in) | Tests every 120V outlet for wiring polarity in 2 seconds |
| Multimeter | Battery voltage, 12V system, DC circuits |
| Telescoping inspection mirror | Behind appliances, undercarriage corners |
| Tape measure | Verify total length, slide-out depth |
| Phone with camera | Document everything — photograph before you write |
| Nitrile gloves | Undercarriage inspection — you will get dirty |
| Knee pads | Undercarriage and floor inspections |
| Ladder (if possible) | Roof walk. Most critical inspection point. |

**Nice to have:** Infrared thermometer (hot spots on electrical), butane leak detector (propane lines), level app on phone.

---

## Ground Rules Before You Touch Anything

1. **Ask the seller to do nothing** — you want to see the trailer cold, not pre-warmed with appliances running
2. **Photograph everything before you open anything** — exterior all four sides, undercarriage, roof if accessible
3. **Run every system yourself** — do not let the seller operate anything. You need to see how things behave, not a curated demo
4. **Take notes in real time** — memory degrades fast when you're looking at 40 items
5. **Walk away if the seller won't let you do a full inspection** — that alone tells you what you need to know

## Section 1: Exterior — Walk-Around

Start outside. Work clockwise around the entire trailer before going in.

### 1A. Overall Condition
- [ ] Note the age, condition, and any obvious collision damage or major repairs
- [ ] Check all four corners — corners are where delamination and water entry begin
- [ ] Look for mismatched paint, patched panels, or replaced trim — signs of prior damage repair
- [ ] Check the hitch coupler: corrosion, cracks, latch function, ball size (1-7/8" or 2")
- [ ] Safety chains: present, rated, properly attached points
- [ ] Jack tongue: hand-crank or electric? Operates smoothly? Foot pad present?
- [ ] Stabilizer jacks: all four present? Crank freely? Pads intact?

### 1B. Exterior Walls
- [ ] Run your hand flat along every wall panel — you're feeling for bubbles, ripples, soft spots
- [ ] **Delamination test:** Press firmly every 12–18 inches. Solid resistance = good. Soft or springy = water has been inside the wall
- [ ] Look down the length of each wall from the corner — waves or bowing visible?
- [ ] Examine every seam where panels meet — caulk intact, no gaps, no staining below seams
- [ ] Window seals: run finger around every window frame — caulk cracked, missing, or pulled away?
- [ ] Check below every window for staining or soft spots — water follows the path of least resistance downward
- [ ] Exterior storage doors: open every one. Hinges intact? Latches work? Water staining inside?
- [ ] Door seals on storage bays: compressed, cracked, or missing?
- [ ] Slide-out walls (if applicable): examine the exterior face of every slide — delamination, seal condition, gap between slide and trailer body

### 1C. Roof
**This is the most important single inspection point on any used RV. Do not skip this.**

If you can safely access it:
- [ ] Walk the entire roof — feel for soft spots with every step (soft = rotted decking underneath)
- [ ] EPDM rubber (most trailers): should be uniformly gray, pliable, and smooth — NOT cracked, brittle, chalky, or torn
- [ ] TPO/fiberglass roofs: look for cracks at seams and around any penetration
- [ ] Every roof penetration (vents, A/C, antennas, solar, pipes): caulked with lap sealant? Any lifting or cracking at the edges?
- [ ] Lap sealant condition overall: dried out and cracking = this roof has not been maintained
- [ ] Seams where roof meets sidewalls: intact, no gaps, no staining on interior sidewall below
- [ ] A/C unit(s): physically secured? Gasket sealing to roof intact? Any rust around the mounting screws?
- [ ] Vents: lids open and close? Screens intact? Inspect the underside once inside

If you cannot access the roof (seller won't allow, no ladder):
- [ ] Use a flashlight at every interior vent and the A/C from below — look up for daylight, water staining, or soft ceiling material
- [ ] Examine the ceiling inside at all four corners and below every roof penetration

### 1D. Undercarriage
Get on the ground and look underneath.
- [ ] Frame rails: **rust is the red flag** — surface rust is normal, pitting or flaking is not
- [ ] Crossmembers: intact, straight, no cracking at weld points
- [ ] Check where the frame attaches to the body — common failure point
- [ ] Underbelly wrap (if present): intact, no tears, no sagging — sagging = water pooling above it
- [ ] Axles: straight, no visible bending or cracks near spring hangers
- [ ] Leaf springs: all leaves present, no cracks, even spacing — look for a leaf that's clearly separated
- [ ] Spring hangers: attached solidly, no cracking or rust through
- [ ] U-bolts on axles: in place and not visibly loose
- [ ] Brake wiring: follow it along the axle — insulation intact, no bare wire
- [ ] Holding tanks: no cracks, no staining below the tank, valves accessible
- [ ] Black tank blade valve: pull the handle — should close completely with minimal force
- [ ] Fresh water tank: visible cracks? Mounting straps secure?
- [ ] Propane lines: no kinking, no corrosion at fittings

### 1E. Tires and Wheels
- [ ] Tire brand and load rating — avoid Chinese ST (Special Trailer) tires, especially Westlake, Taskmaster, Trailer King
- [ ] Age: DOT date code stamped on sidewall, last 4 digits = week/year (e.g., 2819 = 28th week of 2019). **Any tire over 5–6 years old is a liability regardless of tread**
- [ ] Tread depth: minimum 4/32" recommended for highway use
- [ ] Sidewall condition: cracking, dry rot, bulges — any of these = replace immediately
- [ ] Inflation: check with a gauge, not by eye
- [ ] Lug nuts: all present, no obvious looseness
- [ ] Wheels: no cracks, no severe corrosion, valve stems intact
- [ ] Spare tire: present? Mounted securely? Usable condition?

## Section 2: Interior — Systematic Walk-Through

Enter and work from front to back, ceiling to floor.

### 2A. Ceiling
- [ ] Look at the entire ceiling — any staining, discoloration, soft spots, or bubbled material
- [ ] Press gently on the ceiling in corners and below roof penetrations
- [ ] Vent fan covers: intact? Open and close freely?
- [ ] A/C vent: look at the ceiling around it for staining or soft areas

### 2B. Walls
- [ ] Every interior wall — feel for soft spots, especially at the base and around windows
- [ ] Corner moldings: hiding water damage is common here — peel back or press to check what's underneath
- [ ] Slide-out interior walls: check the top of every slide interior for water intrusion (water enters at the top seal and runs down)
- [ ] Behind all furniture: lift cushions, look behind built-in seating — check the wall behind it

### 2C. Floor
- [ ] Walk every square foot — feel for soft, spongy, or springy spots (subfloor water damage)
- [ ] Tap the floor with your heel — solid thud = good, hollow sound = rot or separation
- [ ] Specifically check: entry door area, bathroom floor, base of exterior walls, slide-out floors
- [ ] Lift rugs and floor mats — sellers hide water staining under them
- [ ] Bathroom floor: stand in the center and shift your weight — any movement indicates subfloor failure

### 2D. Slide-Outs
For each slide:
- [ ] Run the slide fully OUT — does it move smoothly? Any grinding, hesitation, or unusual noise?
- [ ] Inspect all four seal strips (top, bottom, both sides) — compressed, intact, not cracked or torn
- [ ] Check the floor where the slide meets the main floor — gap even? Any light visible?
- [ ] Run the slide fully IN — same questions
- [ ] Once retracted: look at the top of the slide box from outside — seal compressed evenly?
- [ ] Any water staining inside the slide box itself (the wall inside the slide)?

### 2E. Windows and Doors
- [ ] Every window: open and close, lock functions, seals intact, no fogging between panes (failed thermal seal)
- [ ] **Fogged windows = moisture between panes** — condensation is permanently trapped; only fix is replacement
- [ ] Entry door: opens, closes, and latches without force — door that has to be forced is telling you the frame has shifted
- [ ] Door seal: compressed, intact around full perimeter
- [ ] Screen door: intact, latches work
- [ ] Any evidence of door frame water intrusion at the bottom corners of the door opening

### 2F. Cabinetry and Storage
- [ ] Open every cabinet and drawer — look for water staining, swelling, delamination of cabinet material
- [ ] Hinges and latches: functional, not broken — broken latches mean cabinets flew open in transit
- [ ] Underneath every sink cabinet: this is a primary water leak point — check carefully for staining, soft wood, mold smell
- [ ] Pantry and wardrobe: check the back wall and floor — water often enters here from exterior walls

## Section 3: Systems — Run Everything

### 3A. Electrical — Shore Power (120V AC)
You need to be plugged in (30A or 50A). Ask the seller to provide power or bring an adapter.
- [ ] Plug in outlet tester at every 120V outlet — should show correct wiring (two amber lights on most testers)
- [ ] Any outlet showing open ground, reversed polarity, or open neutral: document and flag
- [ ] GFCI outlets: press TEST button — outlet should go dead. Press RESET — outlet should restore
- [ ] Breaker panel: open it. Are breakers labeled? Any tripped breakers? Corrosion on bus bars?
- [ ] A/C unit(s): turn on, set to cool, run for 10 minutes — does it blow cold? Any unusual noise?
- [ ] Microwave: run for 30 seconds
- [ ] Refrigerator on AC mode: should be running. Check temp after 30 min if possible
- [ ] Interior lights: every one — check for bulb failures and flickering (12V and 120V)

### 3B. Electrical — Battery / 12V DC System
- [ ] Locate the battery compartment
- [ ] Battery voltage with multimeter: 12.6V+ = fully charged, 12.0V = 50% discharged, below 11.8V = likely dead or failing
- [ ] Terminal connections: clean, tight, no corrosion (white/green powder = corrosion — clean but note it)
- [ ] Battery age: usually a date sticker on top — most RV batteries last 3–5 years
- [ ] Converter/charger: when plugged into shore power, the converter should be charging the battery (voltage should read 13.0–14.4V when plugged in vs. battery-only)
- [ ] 12V lighting: all interior 12V lights functional (these run off battery, not shore power)
- [ ] Water pump (12V): turn on — should pressurize within 2–3 seconds. Runs constantly = leak somewhere in the system
- [ ] Slide-out motors (12V): test on battery power, not just shore power
- [ ] Awning motor (if electric, 12V): deploy and retract

### 3C. Propane / LP System
**Safety first: smell for propane before testing anything. If you smell it, stop and ventilate.**
- [ ] Tank(s): mounted securely in compartment, no visible corrosion on tank body, OPD valve present (triangular hand wheel = OPD compliant)
- [ ] Regulator: located at the tank, connected to supply line — no cracks, no corrosion
- [ ] Supply line from regulator: no kinking, no cracking, no abrasion
- [ ] Open the tank valve slowly
- [ ] Furnace: set thermostat to heat, turn on — should ignite within 3–4 attempts. Let it run 5 minutes. Blows warm air? Cycles off at setpoint?
- [ ] Water heater (propane mode): turn on — ignition within 3–4 attempts, no lockout, pilot or DSI light indicates lit
- [ ] Range/cooktop: ignite every burner — even, stable flame, no yellow tips (yellow = incomplete combustion, possible orifice issue)
- [ ] Oven: ignite and let run 10 minutes
- [ ] Refrigerator (propane mode): switch to LP — should light and begin cooling within minutes
- [ ] CO detector: press test button — should alarm
- [ ] LP detector: press test button — should alarm (mounted low, near the floor, since propane is heavier than air)

### 3D. Plumbing — Fresh Water System
- [ ] Fill the fresh water tank or connect to a city water hookup (preferred — higher pressure reveals leaks)
- [ ] Turn on water pump (if using tank) — listen and watch for drips anywhere under the trailer and under every sink cabinet
- [ ] Run every faucet: hot and cold at kitchen sink, bathroom sink, shower/tub
- [ ] Check water pressure: should feel normal — very low pressure may indicate a failing pump or kinked line
- [ ] Water heater: bypass valve in correct position? Run hot water until it arrives at the faucet
- [ ] Shower pan: check for cracks, look at the drain — sealing intact?
- [ ] Toilet: flush — does it flush cleanly? Any slow drain or backup?
- [ ] Toilet seal: look at the base where it meets the floor — no water staining or soft floor around it
- [ ] Under every sink: with water running, check every drain connection and supply line for drips
- [ ] Outdoor shower (if present): turn on, check for leaks
- [ ] **Black tank flush port (if present):** do not operate — just confirm the fitting cap is present and sealed

### 3E. Plumbing — Drain / Gray & Black System
- [ ] Open every drain valve (gray and black) from the outside — should open fully and close completely
- [ ] Black tank valve: pull and push — closes with a definite stop? Any resistance or binding?
- [ ] Smell test at sewer connection area: sewage smell at the valve when closed = failing blade valve seal
- [ ] Tank monitor panel: switch to tank level — fresh, gray, black — do readings make sense? (Note: black tank sensors are notoriously inaccurate, this is informational only)

## Section 4: Appliances Deep-Check

### 4A. Refrigerator
This is the single most common major appliance failure in used RVs.
- [ ] Mode tested: AC mode AND LP mode
- [ ] Interior temp after 30+ minutes on: should be below 40°F (bring a cheap thermometer)
- [ ] Freezer compartment: is it actually freezing?
- [ ] Door seals: close the door on a piece of paper — should grip firmly all the way around
- [ ] Exterior vent (behind fridge): open the upper and lower exterior vents and look in with a flashlight — look for carbon deposits (black soot) which indicate a burner running too rich or venting problems
- [ ] Any corrosion visible on the cooling unit fins inside the freezer = early cooling unit failure

### 4B. Furnace
- [ ] Ignition: lights within 3–4 cycles?
- [ ] Runs at least 10 minutes without lockout
- [ ] All registers blow warm air — check every duct
- [ ] Exterior furnace vent: look inside with flashlight for carbon buildup, bird nests, wasp nests (common)

### 4C. Water Heater
- [ ] Tested on LP and on electric (6-gallon models often have both)
- [ ] Exterior vent area: look for corrosion, carbon, or any signs of flame rollout
- [ ] Anode rod (if accessible): should be replaced if heavily corroded — ask seller when it was last changed
- [ ] Pressure relief valve: present, not corroded, drain tube pointed down and away from the trailer

### 4D. Air Conditioning
- [ ] Runs without tripping the breaker
- [ ] Blows cold (measure outlet temp if possible — should be 15–20°F below ambient)
- [ ] Filter: pull it and look — clogged filter is a deferred maintenance flag
- [ ] Ceiling gasket (check from the roof): the foam gasket between the A/C unit and the roof opening must be intact — if it's degraded, rain enters the trailer through the A/C opening
- [ ] Fan modes: both high and low speeds work?

### 4E. Awning
- [ ] Manual or electric — fully extend
- [ ] Fabric: no tears, no mold/mildew (look at the underside), seams intact
- [ ] Arms: no bending, locks at each arm engage
- [ ] Pitch: slopes slightly toward the ends to shed water? Flat or reversed pitch will pool water
- [ ] Retract fully — does it roll up evenly?

---

## Section 5: Documentation Review

Ask for all of these before leaving.

- [ ] **Title**: is the seller's name on it? Clean title? No liens? (Run the VIN through your state's DMV or a lien check service)
- [ ] **VIN plate**: located on the tongue or A-frame — matches the title?
- [ ] **Manufacture date sticker**: inside the entry door frame or in a cabinet — exact build month and year. Critical for pre/post-Thor Jayco, pre/post-2019 Grand Design, etc.
- [ ] **RVIA seal**: usually a small metal seal near the entry — indicates it passed factory inspection
- [ ] **Maintenance records**: any documentation of roof resealing, axle service, appliance repairs
- [ ] **Warranty documentation**: any transferable warranty still active?
- [ ] **Owner's manual**: not critical but a nice-to-have

## Red Flag Severity Guide

Not all problems are equal. Categorize what you find:

| Category | What It Means | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Deal-Breaker** | Walk away or demand seller fixes before sale | Active water intrusion, frame rot, soft floor throughout, failed refrigerator cooling unit, title issues, seller won't allow inspection |
| **Major — Negotiate Hard** | Legitimate repair cost deduction from asking price | Delamination on one or more walls, tire replacement needed, black tank valve failure, A/C not cooling, furnace lockout |
| **Moderate — Factor In** | Real cost but manageable | Single soft spot (small), faded/oxidized roof (needs resealing), one failed outlet, cracked vent lid, awning fabric worn |
| **Minor — Accept and Move On** | Normal wear, cosmetic, or cheap to fix | Burned-out bulbs, loose cabinet hinge, cracked corner trim, minor surface rust on frame, toilet seal seeping slightly |
